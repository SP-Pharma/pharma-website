About WHO

The World Health Organization (WHO) is a specialized agency of the United Nations responsible for international public health. It was established in 1948 and works with countries around the world to promote health, keep the world safe, and serve the vulnerable.

WHO provides global leadership on health matters, shapes health research agendas, sets norms and standards, and monitors and assesses health trends. It plays a crucial role in disease outbreak response and health system strengthening.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

What is the role of WHO?

WHO leads global efforts in public health, provides technical support to countries, and monitors international health risks.

How does WHO respond to pandemics?

WHO coordinates international health responses, issues guidelines, and provides support to affected regions during global emergencies.

What types of health data does WHO provide?

WHO offers data on disease burden, vaccination, maternal health, nutrition, and more via its Global Health Observatory.

Where can I access WHO guidelines?

Guidelines are available on the WHO website under the publications section.

What is ICD and how is it used?

ICD (International Classification of Diseases) is used for coding diseases and conditions, essential for health records and statistics.
