The United States Food and Drug Administration (USFDA) is a federal agency of the Department of Health and Human Services. It is responsible for protecting public health by ensuring the safety, efficacy, and security of human and veterinary drugs, biological products, and medical devices.

The USFDA plays a critical role in regulating the nation’s food supply, cosmetics, and products that emit radiation. Through scientific evaluation, regulatory inspections, and public safety communications, the FDA helps maintain trust in medical innovations and ensures that only safe, effective, and high-quality products reach the public.

Stakeholders including pharmaceutical companies, healthcare professionals, and consumers rely on the FDA for guidelines, resources, inspections, and timely approvals to navigate the regulatory landscape.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

What is the role of the FDA?

The FDA ensures the safety, efficacy, and quality of drugs, medical devices, food, and cosmetics in the United States.

How can I check if a drug is FDA-approved?

You can verify approvals using the Drugs@FDA Database, which contains drug labels and approval history.

Where can I report a bad reaction to a drug?

Adverse reactions can be reported through MedWatch, the FDA's reporting system for consumers and professionals.

What’s the process for getting FDA approval?

Companies must submit scientific evidence and clinical trials; the FDA then evaluates and approves based on safety and effectiveness.

What is a Warning Letter?

A warning letter is issued after inspections reveal regulatory violations. It requires corrective action.

How can I find if a product is recalled?

The FDA lists recalls on its website, covering drugs, food, devices, and cosmetics along with reasons and safety instructions.
