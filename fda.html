About USFDA

The United States Food and Drug Administration (USFDA) is a federal agency of the Department of Health and Human Services. It is responsible for protecting public health by ensuring the safety, efficacy, and security of human and veterinary drugs, biological products, medical devices, cosmetics, and food supply.

The FDA also advances public health by helping to speed innovations that make medicines more effective, safer, and more affordable, and by helping the public get accurate, science-based information about medicines and foods to maintain and improve their health.

Main Responsibilities

  • Regulate the development and marketing of drugs, biologics, and medical devices.
  • Ensure the safety of the nation’s food supply and cosmetics.
  • Oversee the safety and labeling of tobacco products.
  • Monitor the manufacturing practices through inspections and compliance.
  • Evaluate new drug applications (NDAs) and generic drugs (ANDAs).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the “FDA Official Site”?

The FDA Official Site refers to the primary web portal maintained by the FDA. It provides authoritative information on regulations, guidance documents, enforcement actions, databases, and public-health communications. It is the central resource for regulated industry, healthcare professionals and consumers to access updates and official FDA policy.

What are “Warning Letters”?

Warning Letters are formal notices the FDA issues to a company or individual when inspections or reviews reveal significant violations of federal laws or regulations. These letters identify the concern(s) and request a corrective-action response within a specified timeframe.

What are “Guidelines”?

Guidelines are documents issued by the FDA that describe the agency’s current thinking on regulatory or compliance matters (such as Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP), clinical trials, labeling etc.). Although not legally binding in the same way as statutes or regulations, they provide detailed advice to industry and help interpret how FDA expects regulated parties to operate.

What are “Drug Databases”?

Drug databases refer to searchable electronic collections maintained or referenced by the FDA that include information on approved drugs, biologics, adverse-event data, safety labels, and other post-market surveillance records. These support transparency and enable stakeholders to find regulatory, safety or compliance information.

What are “Clinical Trials”?

Clinical trials are research studies involving human participants, designed to evaluate the safety and effectiveness of drugs, biologics, or medical devices. The FDA regulates the design, conduct, monitoring, and reporting of clinical trials to ensure the rights, safety and data integrity of participants and to support regulatory decision-making.

What are “Medical Devices”?

Medical devices are instruments, apparatus, implants, software, or related articles intended for use in the diagnosis, prevention, monitoring or treatment of disease or injury in humans. The FDA oversees their regulation throughout development, manufacturing, labeling, distribution and post-market surveillance to protect public health.

What is “ECFR (21 CFR)”?

“ECFR (21 CFR)” refers to the Electronic Code of Federal Regulations containing Title 21 of the Code of Federal Regulations. Title 21 includes rules issued by the FDA governing food, drugs, biologics, devices and other regulated products. Industry uses 21 CFR to locate legally binding regulatory requirements.

What is “Form 483”?

Form 483 (officially “Inspectional Observations”) is a form issued at the end of an FDA inspection when investigators observe conditions or practices that may violate regulatory requirements. The issuance does not represent final agency determination, but signals potential compliance issues that the firm must address.

What is the “Inspection Dashboard”?

The Inspection Dashboard is a data-visualization tool on the FDA’s public portal (for example via the FDA Data Dashboard) that displays inspection statistics: number of inspections by fiscal year, by product category, domestic versus foreign, regulatory outcomes, etc. It enhances transparency into the FDA’s inspections and compliance activities.

What are “Recalls”?

A recall is an action taken by a firm (or at the FDA’s request) to remove a product from the market or correct it because it violates FDA-administered laws or presents a risk to public health. Recalls are classified (Class I-III) based on the severity of risk.

What is “MedWatch”?

MedWatch is the FDA’s Safety Information and Adverse Event Reporting Program. It allows healthcare professionals, consumers and patients to report serious adverse reactions, product quality issues, therapeutic failures, or use errors related to FDA-regulated products. It supports post-market surveillance and timely safety communications.

What is “About FDA”?

“About FDA” refers to the section of the FDA’s website that explains the Agency’s mission, history, organizational structure, roles and responsibilities, and how it protects public health. It gives stakeholders context about the FDA’s regulatory authority and scope.

How does the FDA approve drugs?

Drugs undergo a rigorous evaluation process involving preclinical testing, clinical trials, and review of safety and efficacy data before FDA approval.

How can I report adverse drug reactions?

Reports can be submitted through the FDA’s MedWatch program available on their official website.

What is the role of the FDA in food safety?

The FDA ensures that food products (except for meat, poultry, and certain egg products regulated by USDA) are safe, wholesome, sanitary, and properly labeled.
